Vegan & Plant-Based Nutrition in Australia

A well-planned plant-based diet can meet almost all nutritional needs — but several nutrients require specific attention or supplementation. This guide covers the non-negotiables, in priority order.

The honest summary: One nutrient (B12) requires mandatory supplementation regardless of how well-planned your diet is. Three others (iron, zinc, omega-3) need active dietary strategies. Two more (iodine, calcium) need specific food choices. Get these right and plant-based eating is nutritionally viable for all life stages.

⚡ Vitamin B12 SUPPLEMENT REQUIRED

There is no reliable plant-based source of Vitamin B12. Algae, fermented foods, and nutritional yeast contain B12 analogues that are not biologically active in humans. B12 deficiency develops slowly (the liver stores 2–3 years’ worth) but is devastating when it arrives: irreversible nerve damage, megaloblastic anaemia, cognitive impairment, and neurological symptoms that may be mistaken for dementia in older vegans.

Minimum: 50 µg/day (cyanocobalamin or methylcobalamin) daily, OR 2,000 µg once per week — the high dose compensates for the passive absorption pathway when active absorption is saturated. Choose supplements over fortified foods for reliability.

Cost: a 90-day supply of 1,000µg B12 tablets costs approximately $8–12 at Australian pharmacies. It is the single most important purchase a vegan can make.

🥊 Iron ACTIVE STRATEGY REQUIRED

Plant-based (non-heme) iron is 2–3× less absorbable than heme iron from meat. This is not a reason to avoid plant iron — it is a reason to be strategic about it.

  • Always pair with Vitamin C: A squeeze of lemon, tomato, capsicum, or broccoli alongside lentils, beans, or spinach can increase iron absorption 2–4×. This is the single most effective iron strategy for vegans.
  • Avoid tea and coffee within 1 hour of iron-rich meals. Tannins reduce non-heme iron absorption by 60–90%.
  • Soak and rinse legumes before cooking to reduce phytates by 30–60%, improving iron (and zinc) bioavailability.
  • Eat more total iron: Aim for 1.8× the standard RDI to compensate for lower bioavailability. Women 19–50: ~32mg/day from plant sources (vs 18mg standard RDI).

Best budget plant iron sources: Lentils (3.3mg/100g cooked), canned kidney beans (2.9mg/100g), firm tofu (3mg/100g), fortified breakfast cereal, oats, spinach, pumpkin seeds.

Testing: Ask your GP for a serum ferritin test (not just haemoglobin). Ferritin below 30 µg/L indicates depleted iron stores even before anaemia develops. Female vegans and those who menstruate should test annually.

🦔 Zinc ACTIVE STRATEGY REQUIRED

Phytates in grains, legumes, nuts, and seeds bind zinc in the gut, reducing absorption by 25–50% compared to animal-source zinc. This means vegans need roughly 50% more total dietary zinc to achieve the same absorbed amount.

Strategies to improve zinc absorption:

  • Soak dried legumes overnight and discard water before cooking — reduces phytate content 30–60%
  • Sprouting further reduces phytates and increases zinc bioavailability
  • Sourdough fermentation reduces phytates in bread — a meaningful advantage over regular bread
  • Include pumpkin seeds (8mg/100g) regularly — the highest plant zinc source

Vegan zinc targets: Men 14mg/day × 1.5 = aim for ~21mg from plant sources. Women 8mg/day × 1.5 = aim for ~12mg.

🧒 Omega-3 (DHA and EPA) SUPPLEMENT RECOMMENDED

ALA (alpha-linolenic acid) from flaxseed, chia seeds, walnuts, and hemp seeds is the plant form of omega-3. The problem: conversion to the biologically active DHA and EPA forms is inefficient — approximately 5–15% conversion for DHA, less for EPA. For most healthy adults, adequate ALA intake is sufficient. But for pregnancy, breastfeeding, infants, and those with cardiovascular or cognitive concerns, the conversion may be insufficient.

Recommended: Algae-based DHA/EPA supplement (200–300mg DHA/day). Algae is where fish get their DHA — going directly to the source bypasses the inefficient conversion. Available at Australian health food stores and chemists for ~$20–30/month.

Best budget ALA sources: Ground flaxseed (2.4g ALA per tbsp, ~$0.10/serve), chia seeds (5g per 30g serve), walnuts (2.6g per 30g serve).

🏱 Calcium

Without dairy, calcium requires deliberate food choices. The challenge is that many plant calcium sources contain oxalates (spinach, silverbeet) or phytates (legumes) that significantly reduce calcium absorption.

Strategy: 2× fortified plant milk + calcium-set tofu + kale or bok choy 3×/week covers calcium requirements for most adults. Avoid relying on spinach or silverbeet as a calcium source despite their high listed calcium content — the oxalate binding makes it largely unavailable.

🌎 Iodine SUPPLEMENT USUALLY NEEDED

Seaweed is widely promoted as a vegan iodine source but is actually the worst option — iodine content varies 100-fold between species and even between batches of the same species. Nori can range from 16µg to over 1,000µg per sheet. This variability makes it impossible to reliably dose from seaweed.

Reliable options: Use iodised salt (use modestly — only 1/4 tsp covers the 150µg target but sodium intake must be managed), OR take a kelp supplement standardised to ~150µg iodine per day. Vegans who avoid dairy and fish are at particular risk of deficiency.

☀️ Vitamin D

Same requirements as omnivores. Sun exposure covers requirements for most Australians most of the year (15–30 min daily on arms and legs). In winter in southern states, or for those with limited sun exposure, a supplement of 1,000–2,000 IU/day is appropriate. Ensure any supplement is labelled D3 (cholecalciferol) from lichen — not D2 — for best bioavailability.

Protein on a vegan diet

Vegan protein sources are nutritionally adequate but two adjustments are needed:

1. Eat 10–20% more total protein than the standard RDI to compensate for lower digestibility of plant proteins (DIAAS scores typically 0.5–0.8 vs 1.0–1.2 for animal proteins).
2. Combine protein sources across the day to cover all essential amino acids. Legumes (high in lysine, low in methionine) + grains (high in methionine, low in lysine) complement each other. Rice and lentils, beans on toast, hummus with bread — these traditional pairings are nutritionally sound.

Best budget vegan protein sources: Cooked lentils (9g/100g), firm tofu (8g/100g), cooked chickpeas (8.9g/100g), oats dry (17g/100g), peanut butter (25g/100g), tempeh (19g/100g).

Life stages requiring extra attention

Pregnancy and breastfeeding: Mandatory B12 supplement + algae DHA (300mg/day) + iron monitoring (serum ferritin testing recommended each trimester) + iodine supplement (150µg/day in addition to diet). Inform your GP or midwife. See the Pregnancy Nutrition guide.
Children and teenagers: B12 mandatory. Iron and zinc monitoring strongly recommended. Calcium from fortified plant milk (3 serves/day) is critical during growth. See Kids & Toddlers and the Life Stages guide.
Older adults: B12 supplement (gastric acid decline makes food-based B12 unreliable regardless of source). Increase protein to 1.2–1.6 g/kg/day. Algae DHA for cognitive and cardiovascular protection. See the Life Stages guide for the full older adult protein story.
